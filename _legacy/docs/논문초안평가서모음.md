## 1. Summary

This manuscript introduces QNS (Quantum Noise Symbiote), a framework for noise-adaptive qubit placement in NISQ-era quantum circuits. The core idea is to exploit spatial heterogeneity in two-qubit gate fidelities by remapping logical qubits to physical qubits such that frequently-used logical pairs are assigned to high-fidelity hardware edges. The authors claim up to 90% fidelity improvement for circuits executing on low-fidelity edges, with sub-millisecond optimization times. An ablation study suggests placement optimization itself contributes ~75.5% of total fidelity gains. The work is implemented in Rust with Python bindings and validated through analytical modeling and Monte Carlo simulation.

---

## 2. Overall Assessment & Recommendation

**Recommendation: Major Revision Required** (with potential for significant impact if validation gaps are addressed)

The manuscript addresses an important and timely problem in NISQ computingâ€”exploiting hardware heterogeneity for compilation. The premise is sound, and the early results are promising. However, **critical experimental and methodological gaps** prevent publication in its current form. The work lacks validation on **real quantum hardware**, which is essential for any NISQ optimization paper claiming practical utility. Additionally, the evaluation framework is incomplete: baseline comparisons against state-of-the-art transpilers are insufficient, topology constraints are not adequately addressed, and statistical rigor is weak. The manuscript would be suitable for npj Quantum Information only if these fundamental concerns are resolved.

---

## 3. Major Concerns

### 3.1. Lack of Real Hardware Validation

**Critical Issue:** The entire validation relies on analytical modeling and Monte Carlo simulation (100 shots). For a paper claiming "90% fidelity improvement" and positioning itself for "real-time integration," this is insufficient.

- **Required Action:** Execute **at least 3-5 representative circuits** (e.g., GHZ state preparation, QFT, VQE ansatz) on **actual IBM or Googledevices**. Use the latest calibrated devices (e.g., IBM Heron, Google Sycamore). Compare QNS-optimized circuits against:
  - Identity mapping
  - Qiskit `transpile()` with optimization level 3 and `FakeBackend` noise-adaptive mapping
  - t|ketâŸ©'s `NoiseAwarePlacement`

- **Expected Metrics:** Report **score-based fidelity** (HVOL, OVL), **measurement outcome distributions**, and **statistical significance** across **â‰¥1,000 shots per circuit**. Show that improvements are not washed out by readout errors and crosstalk.

- **If Hardware Access is Limited:** The authors must explicitly state this limitation and downgrade claims from "validated" to "simulation-based proof-of-concept."

### 3.2. Weak Baseline Comparisons

**Issue:** The manuscript compares QNS only against "identity mapping" and vaguely references Qiskit/Cirq without concrete benchmarks.

- **Required Action:** Provide **head-to-head numerical comparisons** with Qiskit's `SabreSwap` and `DenseLayout`, Cirq's `greedy_qubit_mapper`, and t|ketâŸ©'s `NoiseAwarePlacement` on **identical hardware profiles**. Report:
  - Final circuit fidelity (not just improvement over identity)
  - Circuit depth increase (if any)
  - SWAP count
  - Compilation latency

- **Current Gap:** Table 1's "Mixed" and "Complex" scenarios show 0% improvement, but it's unclear if this is due to QNS limitations or already-optimal identity mapping. Baseline comparisons would clarify this.

### 3.3. Topology Constraints Severely Limit Practicality

**Issue:** The methods section admits the implementation is limited to linear/ring topologies. Modern processors (IBM heavy-hex, Google 2D grid) have non-trivial connectivity requiring SWAP routing even after optimal placement.

- **Required Action:** Demonstrate **SWAP insertion logic** for **heavy-hex topology** (IBM Eagle/Heron). Show how QNS placement integrates with routing. If placement alone cannot handle arbitrary topologies, the manuscript must:
  - Explicitly state this limitation in the **Abstract** and **Introduction**
  - Provide complexity analysis for **joint placement+routing**
  - Show at least **one realistic topology benchmark** (e.g., 5-qubit subgraph of IBM heavy-hex)

- **Impact:** Without this, the claim of "real-time integration" is unsupported for production hardware.

### 3.4. Statistical Rigor is Insufficient

**Issue:** The manuscript reports p < 0.001 from **only 5 runs** and uses **100-shot Monte Carlo simulations**, which is inadequate for reliable fidelity estimation.

- **Required Action:**
  - Increase Monte Carlo shots to **â‰¥10,000** for stable fidelity estimates
  - Increase independent runs to **â‰¥30** for statistical tests
  - Report **confidence intervals** (e.g., 95% CI) for all fidelity improvements
  - Use **bootstrapping** to verify CI robustness

- **Specific Problem:** Table 2 shows Î” = 11.59% for GHZ chain (Identity case), which is alarmingly large and suggests simulation variance is high. This undermines trust in the analytical model.

### 3.5. Fidelity Model Oversimplification

**Issue:** The fidelity model (Equation 1) multiplies gate fidelities and exponential decay, ignoring:
- **Correlated errors** (e.g., crosstalk, spectator errors)
- **State-dependent errors** (idle vs. dynamic)
- **Readout errors** (which dominate in shallow circuits)
- **T1/T2 variations** across qubits (assumed uniform)

- **Required Action:**
  - Justify the model's validity for **shallow NISQ circuits** (< 50 gates)
  - Compare against **full Lindblad master equation** simulation for small circuits to quantify model error
  - Discuss how readout errors affect claimed improvements (if using post-readout fidelity, explain clearly)

## 4. Minor Concerns

### 4.1. Ablation Study Methodology
- **Clarity:** Table 3's "Contribution" percentages are ambiguous. Are they relative to the *best* co-optimized case or *worst* identity case? Provide absolute fidelity values for each ablated configuration.
- **Gate Reordering:** The 11.6% contribution is surprisingly high for a simple commutation-based scheduler. Provide an example circuit where this yields >10% fidelity gain.

### 4.2. Implementation Details
- **Scalability:** The manuscript claims O(n) scaling but shows benchmarks only up to 5 qubits/30 gates. Show scaling up to **â‰¥50 qubits/200 gates** to support "NISQ-scale" claim.
- **Beam Search Width:** The choice of width=20 is arbitrary. Provide a sensitivity analysis (width = 5, 10, 20, 50) showing fidelity vs. runtime tradeoffs.

### 4.3. Writing & Presentation
- **Terminology:** "Symbiote" is non-standard and not defined. Use conventional terms like "noise-adaptive placement" to avoid confusion.
- **References:** Missing recent works:
  - Gushu et al., "Quantum Circuit Optimization with AlphaTensor" (2023)
  - Tomesh et al., "SupermarQ: A Scalable Quantum Benchmark Suite" (2022)
  - Liu et al., "QUAFF: Quantum Architecture Fidelity Framework" (2023)
- **Figure Quality:** Figures are referenced as external PDFs. Embed **high-resolution vector graphics** directly in the manuscript.
